Why I Want to Build a SaaS

December 08, 2025 • 4 min read

The Humble Ambition of a Tiny Bit of Code. A reflection on building software for leverage, not scale.

Most conversations about startups revolve around scale, valuations, and the sheer audacity of the vision. That’s fine if you’re trying to build the next social network. But what if your ambition is much smaller, and therefore, perhaps, more honest? My ambition is to build a SaaS business, and it starts not with a grand thesis about the future, but with a simple observation about the present: small and medium businesses (SMBs) are still running on software that feels like it was written in the nineties.

The real prize isn't replacing Microsoft. It’s solving a messy, specific problem for ten thousand plumbers, accountants, or local bakeries.

I’m new to the specifics of SaaS—I’m just now understanding the power of recurring revenue and the difference between a product you sell once and a service you iterate on forever. But the core concept hits me like an epiphany: software is the ultimate form of leverage. As a coder, you can sit down, type a few thousand lines of Python, and that tiny bit of code can instantly solve a scheduling nightmare or a billing headache for someone in a different timezone while you sleep. You’re not charging for your time anymore; you’re charging for the solution’s utility.

My technical foundation is humble: I know Python, and my HTML/CSS skills are just enough to make a button look mildly appealing. Five years ago, that would have been a dead end. But this is where the new generation of builders gets a different kind of leverage.

The barrier to entry for building great software has crumbled. We don't need expensive licenses. We need PostgreSQL and Redis, and the entire open-source ecosystem is waiting, ready for us to use for free. The real game-changer is the way AI, specifically tools like Gemini and ChatGPT, act as a co-pilot. They don’t write the product; they turn the 10-hour task of debugging CSS or figuring out an obscure API into a 10-minute prompt. They multiply the solo founder's output by a factor of ten.

This changes the calculus. I don't need a team of five developers. I need an idea, tenacity, and a powerful LLM to handle the grunt work.

The path to finding an idea isn’t brainstorming; it’s observation. It's looking at an SMB and seeing the ridiculous, manual steps they take to get things done, and realizing a simple web form could fix 80% of their misery. That frustration, that friction—that is the gold mine.

Ultimately, I want to build a SaaS because it’s the purest form of building something useful. You can’t lie about utility when your revenue is recurring; customers will cancel if your software doesn’t solve a real problem every single month. It forces you to build well, to focus ruthlessly on what matters, and to make the user experience as frictionless as possible. That is the kind of constraint that leads to great work. It’s not about getting rich; it’s about making things that people actually need.
